Homework 1: Statistics in CyberSecurity

Exploring the role of statistics in cybersecurity and how modern intrusion detection and prevention systems apply statistical models for anomaly detection.

Assignment

Publish the following short research: “What is statistics, and why can it be useful for cybersecurity?”

Definition and Scope of Statistics

Statistics is the scientific discipline concerned with the collection, description, analysis, interpretation and presentation of data. It provides mathematical frameworks and procedures to characterise variability, to make principled inferences from samples to populations, and to quantify uncertainty through probability models.

From a methodological viewpoint, statistics supplies both descriptive tools (e.g. summary measures, distributions, visualisations) and inferential tools (e.g. hypothesis testing, confidence intervals, regression and probabilistic modelling) that allow practitioners to extract reliable information from noisy measurements.

Why Statistics is Useful for Cybersecurity

Cybersecurity datasets are inherently high-volume, noisy, and non-stationary: network flows, packet headers, system logs and telemetry streams contain variability across users, time, and use-cases. Statistical methods are essential in cybersecurity for several reasons:

  • Baselining normal behaviour: Statistical profiling allows systems to construct formal models of routine activity (e.g. average connection rates, byte distributions, typical port usage) and to quantify deviations from those baselines.
  • Anomaly quantification: Probability models let analysts compute the likelihood of observed events under a learned normal model and flag unusual events that merit investigation.
  • Dimensionality reduction and feature extraction: Techniques such as principal component analysis (PCA), clustering and information-theoretic measures help reduce noise and highlight the signal in high-dimensional telemetry.
  • Robust decision-making under uncertainty: Statistical hypothesis testing, confidence intervals, and Bayesian inference provide disciplined ways to trade off false positives and false negatives and to update beliefs as new evidence arrives.
  • Model evaluation: Cross-validation, ROC curves, precision/recall measures and other statistical metrics are the standard way to validate detection systems and compare design choices.

How IDS/IPS Use Statistical Methods

IDS (Intrusion Detection Systems) and IPS (Intrusion Prevention Systems) historically relied on two broad families of detection methodologies: signature/misuse detection and anomaly-based detection. Signature detection matches traffic against known patterns; anomaly detection uses statistical models to identify behaviours that diverge from established norms.

Anomaly-based IDS/IPS typically implement one or more of the following statistical approaches:

  • Thresholding on statistical aggregates: Comparing packet rates or failed logins against dynamic thresholds derived from historical percentiles or moving averages.
  • Probabilistic models: Using Gaussian models or kernel density estimation to compute likelihoods and flag low-probability events.
  • Time-series analysis: Employing autoregressive models and change-point detection to identify shifts in traffic patterns.
  • Multivariate techniques: Using PCA or multivariate control charts to detect coordinated deviations across multiple features.
  • Clustering and unsupervised learning: Grouping normal behaviours and identifying outliers as potential intrusions.
  • Statistical ensembles: Combining outputs from multiple detectors to create robust anomaly scores.

Statistical Methods in Modern NGFWs

Next-generation firewalls (NGFWs) integrate stateful packet inspection, application awareness, threat intelligence and embedded IDS/IPS engines. Modern NGFWs augment signature rules with statistical and heuristic modules to detect behaviour that signatures cannot capture—for example, zero-day exploit attempts, low-and-slow reconnaissance, and distributed scanning.

  • Rate and volume-based detection: Comparing live traffic to learned baselines for volumetric anomalies.
  • Entropy and distributional checks: Using entropy of payloads or destinations to detect irregularities.
  • Sequence and timing analysis: Examining packet inter-arrival times for tunnelling behaviour.
  • Adaptive thresholding: Dynamically adjusting thresholds based on network conditions.
  • Integration with signature engines: Correlating statistical alerts with known signatures and intelligence feeds.

Analytical Context

These data demonstrate how statistical modelling, including baseline construction, rate-based anomaly detection, and probabilistic inference forms the foundation of intrusion detection in modern NGFW and IDS/IPS products.

Current research trends focus on hybrid systems where statistical methods integrate with machine learning for adaptive tuning, thresholding, and concept drift management.

Conclusion

Statistics provides the foundational language and tools that make anomaly-based intrusion detection possible. In modern NGFWs, statistical methods complement signature detection to extend coverage to previously unseen or polymorphic threats. Implementing statistical IDS/IPS requires careful attention to baseline construction, thresholding, evaluation metrics, and operational maintenance to manage false positives and model drift.
