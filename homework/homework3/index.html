1. What is RSA?

RSA (Rivest-Shamir-Adleman) is a widely used public-key cryptographic system that enables secure data transmission. It relies on the mathematical properties of large prime numbers and modular arithmetic.

In RSA, two keys are generated: a public key for encryption and a private key for decryption. The security of RSA is based on the difficulty of factoring the product of two large prime numbers.

2. RSA Key Generation

How the keys are computed

  1. Random primes p and q: Two distinct small primes are randomly selected to ensure \( n = p \cdot q \geq 29 \).
  2. Modulus n: \( n = p \cdot q \)
  3. Totient φ(n): \( \varphi(n) = (p-1)(q-1) \)
  4. Public exponent e: Smallest coprime with \( \varphi(n) \) from {3,5,17,257}.
  5. Private exponent d: Modular inverse of e, \( d \equiv e^{-1} \pmod{\varphi(n)} \).

3. Encryption (using Public Key)

Encryption uses the public key (n, e) to transform plaintext into ciphertext.
