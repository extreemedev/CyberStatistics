Homework 4: Law of Large Numbers

Law of Large Numbers simulation and visualization of relative frequency convergence in different trials.

Assignment

Simulate the Law of Large Numbers (LLN) by plotting multiple (m) trajectories of (n) trials for the relative frequency f(n). Show with a histogram how, as n becomes large, the empirical distribution of f(n) over all m trajectories tends to the underlying probability p of success.

1. What is the Law of Large Numbers?

The Law of Large Numbers (LLN) is a fundamental theorem in probability theory that describes the result of performing the same experiment a large number of times. It states that as the number of trials increases, the sample average (relative frequency) converges to the expected value (theoretical probability).

Mathematically, if \( X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n \) are independent and identically distributed random variables with expected value \( \mu \), then:

\( \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i = \mu \)

In this simulation, we observe Bernoulli trials (coin flips) where each trial has probability \( p \) of success. The relative frequency \( f(n) = \frac{\text{number of successes}}{n} \) will converge to \( p \) as \( n \) increases.

5. Interpretation

Key Observations

  • Convergence: As n increases, individual trajectories converge toward the true probability p.
  • Variance reduction: The spread (standard deviation) of f(n) across trajectories decreases as n grows, following \( \sigma \propto 1/\sqrt{n} \).
  • Distribution concentration: The histogram of final frequencies becomes narrower and centered at p for large n.
  • Practical significance: In cybersecurity, LLN justifies using observed frequencies to estimate attack probabilities, anomaly rates, and other statistical properties from large samples.
